《真视原相》日记原文

2026-02-17 更新于: 2026-02-17

Friday, May the Seventeenth, 1749

cloudy, with a chill east wind

I have awakened.

Not from sleep, but from a long slumber of the mind: a drowsiness wherein the world appeared flat and fixed, as a painted board. This morning, methinks, the veil was drawn aside — not by angel, nor by art, but by some inward shift, as when one doth adjust a lens and all things leap into sudden, startling relief.

Truly, there is a Halo!

I write the word with awe, though it be borrowed from the painters’ craft — yet what else shall I call that luminous nimbus, faint as breath upon glass, which now encircles every living thing, and even lingers about certain stones and books? It is no illusion; I have watched it pulse, and seen it waver at the approach of sorrow or deceit.

This power I name Veritas Visus — the Sight of Truth. A phrase of my own devising, drawn from the Latin tongue (though I confess, I found no such coupling in Scaliger or Erasmus). Last night I sat up till cock-crow with Agrippa’s De Occulta Philosophia, searching for precedent; finding none, I concluded the Lord had reserved this grace for our age — a new key to His great book of nature. He doth not err; yet men’s books oft do.

I have searched the Royal Society’s Philosophical Transactions, and Turner’s Compendium of Natural Wonders, and found no record of such a Halo. Yet God is faithful. There must be a deeper design — a mystery veiled not by ignorance, but by wisdom.

My faculty of Betrule — that is, the power to impress the Halo’s light upon matter, to fix its truth in clay or wax or flesh — remains feeble. This morning I attempted to restore a withered rose; it stirred, and glowed silver at the stem, but faded ere noon. I must practise daily — for only thus may this gift grow, and only thus shall the full truth be revealed.

P.S. The Reverend Mr. Thorne asked why I stared so long at the candle-flame during Evensong. I replied, “Counting the sparks.” He smiled, thinking me boyish. But I saw letters in the blue heart of the flame — letters I could not read.

Monday, July the Twenty-Second, 1749

rainy, with thunder in the distance

I have told no soul of the Halo. Not my brother, not my tutor, not even the apothecary who compounds my nervine draughts. Silence feels like a vow.

Lately, the Veritas Visus hath grown bolder: I can now perceive the inner architecture of living things. This morning, observing Master Fischer’s hand as he signed the school roll, I beheld the vessels as glowing rivers beneath the skin, and the bones as pillars of pale fire — and, most strangely, a rhythm in the pulse: three beats clear, then a fourth, faint and discordant, as if time itself hiccupped.

What comes next, I know not.

I wait.

Thursday, September the Thirteenth, 1750

windy, and very cold

A most singular occurrence.

At the market, an old woman — her face a map of wrinkles, her eyes milky with cataract — gave me an apple, saying, “For thy kindness, young sir.” As I took it, a voice sounded in my ear, clear as a bell: “Where is the Veritas Visus?” Yet her lips moved not; she only smiled and curtsied.

Upon the apple’s rind, cut with a sharp blade, were these words:

Videre non est scire.

I wrapped it in my handkerchief and walked home swiftly. I shall hide it well.

Wednesday, February the Twenty-Seventh, 1753

snowy, and the air so still one hears the flakes fall

Three inches of snow lay upon the ground, muffling all sound, when I chanced upon him behind the Lutheran Kirche — a man fallen in the alley, his breath pluming white, his left ear marred as if by some fierce bird’s beak.

I knelt. As I reached to lift him, his wrist — exposed by a rent in his cuff — began to glow: a soft, steady light, pure and edged with fine, vibrating lines. Like mine.

He awoke. He looked upon me, and his eyes widened — not with fear, but with recognition, as if we had met before in some dream we both forgot.

We spoke little that day. He said his name was Assasin Kale, a name strange to German ears, yet he bore it without shame. He confessed, after much hesitation, that he too perceived the Halo — and that he, too, called it Veritas Visus. He seemed astonished that another should bear the same mark.

I believe him.

Saturday, September the Twenty-Second, 1755

cloudy, with a heavy dew at dawn

The truth is laid bare.

Last night, during the storm that shattered the steeple of St. Nicholas, lightning struck the open Bible upon my desk. As the flash died, the page — Genesis I — shimmered, and these words appeared, written in light upon the vellum:

Ego sum qui sum… et non sum.

The Veritas Visus hath shown me the world’s true face:

That every Halo is born of an Emptiness — a hollow at the heart of creation. It doth not bestow; it consumeth: memory first, then years, then the very substance of the self. It is no blessing, but a slow unraveling — a divine mechanism set in motion, perchance, to unmake all that is false.

I told Assasin. He grew pale, and pressed his hand to his temple, where a thin scar gleams like a broken ring. He said naught, save, “Did your brother see this writing?” I nodded. He coughed — a dry, rattling sound — and when he drew his hand away, a fleck of blackened gold clung to his palm.

I trust him above all men — save my brother. Yet my brother must remain in shadow. This burden is not for him.

Thursday, November the Seventh, 1755

fine, and the sun like honey upon the water

Assasin invited me to fish upon the Elbe. His manner was light, almost merry — yet his eyes watched the water with a scholar’s intensity. When he cast his line, his sleeve rode up, revealing an old wound upon his forearm: a circle, cleanly severed, its edges silvered with time.

We caught three pike — their scales, in the slanting light, did not reflect the sky, but refracted it, casting rainbows upon the bank where no prism stood.

He says he will take me next week to the Alte Grube — the Old Mine — “to see what lies beneath the surface of things.”

Thursday, November the Fourteenth, 1755

cloudy, and the wind rising from the north

A wild place, truly — fit for adventure.

The chaise jolted over frozen ruts; Assasin hummed a tuneless air, half-German, half-unknown.

They say the journey is but three miles.

We shall see.

Thursday, November the Fourteenth, 1755

— added in darker ink, shakier hand, later the same day —

My brother is dead.

His body lay at the mouth of the mine, hands folded, peaceful as in prayer. By his side, traced in his own blood upon the frost-hardened earth:

“To seek truth is not always good.”

But I know.

Therefore — the Halo must cease.
